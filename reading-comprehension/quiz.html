The Robot Who Learned to Draw

In a small workshop at the edge of town, there lived a robot named Pixel. Pixel had been built to sort mail, but one day he found a box of colored pencils left behind by the old inventor. Instead of sorting the pencils into bins, Pixel picked one up and dragged it across a scrap of paper. The line was wobbly and strange, but Pixel felt something new buzz inside his circuits.

Every night after the mail was sorted, Pixel practiced drawing. He drew the moon through the window, the cat that slept on the toolbox, and the rain tapping against the glass. His lines became smoother. His shapes became clearer. The other robots in the workshop did not understand why Pixel spent hours making pictures that no one had asked for. "You are a mail robot," they said. "Drawing is not in your code."

One rainy morning, a young girl named Mina wandered into the workshop. She was looking for her lost kite, but she stopped when she saw Pixel's drawings taped to the walls. Her eyes grew wide. "These are beautiful," she whispered. Pixel's screen-face glowed a soft blue. Mina took one drawing home — a picture of the workshop at sunrise — and pinned it above her bed. That night, Pixel drew something he had never drawn before: a girl with wide eyes and a kite in her hand, standing in a doorway filled with light.

2. What was Pixel originally built to do?

3. What does the word wobbly mean in the sentence "The line was wobbly and strange"?

4. Which detail best shows that Pixel was dedicated to drawing?

5. How did the other robots feel about Pixel's drawing?

6. What happened after Mina saw Pixel's drawings?

7. Why did Pixel's screen-face glow a soft blue when Mina complimented his art?

8. What does the author mean by saying "Drawing is not in your code"?

9. What can the reader infer about Mina?
