The Silent Garden

At the edge of the village stood a walled garden that no one had entered for over fifty years. Ivy crawled over the stone walls like green veins, and the iron gate had rusted into a permanent yawn. Children dared one another to peek through the bars, but none ever climbed over. The garden belonged, in local memory, to an elderly botanist named Elias Thorne, who had vanished one autumn morning without a word.

Thorne had spent decades collecting rare plants from distant continents. Neighbors recalled the fragrances that once drifted above the wall — jasmine at dusk, citrus at noon, something earthy and unfamiliar after rain. Then, abruptly, the scents stopped. The gate was locked. The windows of the small cottage inside were shuttered, and the path was swallowed by weeds.

Years later, a letter arrived at the village post office, addressed simply to The Garden. It contained a single seed and a note in Thorne’s handwriting: Some things must wait for the right soil. The seed was planted by the village schoolteacher in a pot on her windowsill. Within a month, it had grown into a flower no one could name — pale blue petals that opened only at twilight and released a scent that reminded every villager of something different: a childhood kitchen, a distant shoreline, a voice they had not heard in years.

1. What is the passage mainly about?

2. Why had no one entered the garden for over fifty years?

3. What does the word swallowed mean in the sentence “the path was swallowed by weeds”?

4. Which detail best suggests that Elias Thorne valued patience?

5. How did the villagers most likely feel when the scents from the garden stopped?

6. According to the passage, what happened after the seed was planted?

7. The flower’s scent reminded each villager of something different. What does this imply?

8. Why does the author describe the iron gate as “a permanent yawn”?

9. What can the reader infer about the village schoolteacher?

10. Which statement best expresses the theme of the passage?
