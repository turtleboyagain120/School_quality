Reading Comprehension

Understand more. Remember longer. Think deeper.

What is Reading Comprehension?

Reading comprehension is the ability to process text, understand its meaning, and integrate it with what you already know. It is not just about recognizing words — it is about grasping ideas, making connections, and drawing conclusions.

Strong readers ask themselves questions as they read. They predict what will happen next, visualize scenes, and evaluate the author's purpose.

Example Passage

Every morning, Aria walked past the old clock tower on her way to school. The hands had stopped at 11:47 decades ago, yet the townspeople left it untouched. To Aria, the frozen clock felt like a secret kept by the city — a silent reminder that some moments are meant to be remembered, not measured.

Two Kinds of Questions

Comprehension questions usually fall into two categories:

  • Explicit (Literal): Answers are found directly in the text.
  • Implicit (Inferential): Answers require reading between the lines.

Explicit Example: What time do the clock hands show?

Answer: 11:47 — stated directly in the passage.

Implicit Example: Why might the townspeople leave the clock untouched?

Answer: They probably value it as a historical symbol or collective memory. — not directly stated, but strongly suggested.
